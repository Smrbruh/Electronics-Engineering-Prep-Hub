Build an unshakeable foundation in electrical theory, core components, circuit laws, and essential measurement tools. This section covers the complete landscape from electron physics to practical breadboard assembly.

Lecture 1

Introduction, Environment Setup & Electronic Components

A foundational overview of the course, lab environment setup, a brief history of electronics from 1899 to today, and a complete introduction to all fundamental electronic components and their schematic symbols.

Brief History of Electronics

Modern electronics traces its origins to 1899 when J. J. Thomson at the University of Cambridge discovered the electron, establishing the physical foundation for all electronic devices. Cathode rays, once thought to be waves, were revealed to be streams of negatively charged particles moving from cathode to anode under high voltage.

Key Historical Milestones

  • 1895–1905: Electron discovery (Thomson), X-rays, radioactivity, and early wireless experiments.
  • 1901: Guglielmo Marconi received the first transatlantic wireless Morse-code signal from Cornwall, England to Newfoundland, Canada — proving radio waves travel beyond the horizon.
  • 1906: Lee De Forest invented the Audion vacuum tube (later the triode): filament (cathode) + plate (anode) + control grid. A tiny grid voltage controlled a much larger current — the birth of amplification.
  • 1906: Greenleaf W. Pickard demonstrated the crystal radio detector using galena and a "cat's whisker" wire — the first hint of semiconductor behavior.
  • 1920: Station KDKA in Pittsburgh launched commercial radio broadcasting.
  • 1946: ENIAC, the world's first large-scale electronic digital computer, was dedicated at the University of Pennsylvania.
  • December 1947: Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley (Bell Labs) demonstrated the first transistor using germanium. Nobel Prize in Physics 1956.
  • 1958: Jack Kilby (Texas Instruments) and Robert Noyce (Fairchild) independently developed the Integrated Circuit. Nobel Prize in Physics 2000.
  • 1971: Intel introduced the 4004 microprocessor — 2,300 transistors with 10 μm features. Modern Xeon chips exceed 6 billion transistors at under 10 nm.

Must Remember

The evolution follows: Vacuum Tube → Transistor → Integrated Circuit → Microprocessor. Each generation made electronics smaller, faster, cheaper, and more reliable.

Analog vs. Digital Electronics

All electronics falls into two fundamental categories that determine how signals are represented and processed.

Digital Electronics

Operates with a limited number of discrete states — almost always just two: LOW (0) and HIGH (1). Voltage ranges define these states:

  • 0 V to 0.4 V → LOW (binary 0)
  • 2.0 V to 5.0 V → HIGH (binary 1)

A digital circuit treats 2 V and 4 V identically because both fall in the HIGH range. Examples: microprocessors, logic gates, counters, memory.

Analog Electronics

Responds to or produces outputs with a continuous range of values. A signal may vary between 0 V and 10 V and could be 1.5 V, 2.8 V, or 7.653 V — theoretically infinitely many possible levels. Examples: amplifiers, filters, oscillators, sensors.

ADC Quantization Levels

When converting analog to digital, the number of discrete levels is:

Levels = 2ⁿ

where n is the number of bits. A 5-bit ADC gives 32 levels; an 8-bit ADC gives 256 levels; a 16-bit ADC gives 65,536 levels.

Exam Note

Know the difference between Circuit A (ADC: analog → digital), Circuit B (amplifier: small AC → larger AC), and Circuit C (rectifier/shaper: AC → pulses). These circuit types appear frequently in exams.

Schematic & Block Diagrams

Schematic Diagram

Shows every individual component in a circuit and all interconnections using standardized symbols. Required for component-level troubleshooting — isolating and replacing a single defective resistor, capacitor, or IC.

Block Diagram

Shows all functional units of a system and how signals flow between them, without specifying individual components. Used for system-level understanding — for example, a power supply block diagram might show: Transformer → Rectifier → Filter → Regulator → Load.

Engineering Tip

Always start troubleshooting from the block diagram to isolate the faulty subsystem, then use the schematic to pinpoint the bad component. Block first, then schematic.

Resistors

A resistor is a passive electronic component that limits or controls the flow of electric current in a circuit. It is the most fundamental passive component in electronics. The unit of resistance is the Ohm (Ω).

What Resistors Do

  • Reduce current flow (current limiting)
  • Drop voltage across themselves (voltage division)
  • Protect sensitive components (e.g., limiting current into an LED)
  • Control signal levels and set bias points
  • Convert current into measurable voltage (shunt resistors)

Types of Fixed Resistors

Wirewound Resistance wire on ceramic core Very high power handling Power electronics, motor drives
SMD (Surface Mount) Thick/thin film on ceramic chip Tiny, suited for PCB reflow Consumer electronics, mobile devices

Variable Resistors

Potentiometer (3-Terminal)

A potentiometer has three terminals: Terminal 1 (one end of resistive track), Terminal 2 — the movable wiper, and Terminal 3 (other end of resistive track). Turning the wiper changes the ratio of R1 to R2 along the track.

Function: Acts as a voltage divider. Output voltage is proportional to wiper position.

Vout = Vin × R₂ / (R₁ + R₂)

Applications: Audio volume control, brightness/contrast adjustment, calibration circuits, joystick position sensing.

Rheostat (2-Terminal)

A rheostat uses only two of the potentiometer's three terminals — one end and the wiper — making it a simple variable series resistance. Rheostats typically handle higher currents than potentiometers.

Applications: Motor speed control, light dimmers, heating element control.

Safety Warning

Never exceed a resistor's power rating (Watts). An under-rated resistor will overheat, smoke, and possibly catch fire. Always derate by at least 50% in continuous-duty applications (e.g., use a 1 W resistor where calculations show 0.5 W is needed).

Troubleshooting — Resistors

  • Measure resistance with the component powered off and at least one leg lifted from the circuit — in-circuit measurement gives false readings due to parallel paths.
  • A burned or discolored resistor almost certainly has the wrong power rating for the application.
  • An open-circuit reading (OL/overload on DMM) means the resistor has failed open.
  • A near-zero reading means the resistor has failed short — rare but possible with wirewound types.

Capacitors

A capacitor is a passive electronic component that stores electrical energy in an electric field between two metal plates separated by an insulating material called the dielectric. The unit of capacitance is the Farad (F).

What Capacitors Do

  • Store and release energy quickly (energy buffer)
  • Block DC while allowing AC to pass (coupling)
  • Smooth rectified voltage (filter capacitor in power supplies)
  • Form timing circuits with resistors (RC networks)
  • Tune resonant circuits with inductors (LC tanks)
  • Bypass high-frequency noise to ground (decoupling)

Key Capacitor Formulas

  • Q = C × V — Charge stored = Capacitance × Voltage
  • E = ½ × C × V² — Energy stored in joules
  • Xc = 1 / (2π × f × C) — Capacitive reactance (opposition to AC)
  • τ = R × C — RC time constant (time to charge to 63.2%)
  • Series: 1/C_total = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + …
  • Parallel: C_total = C₁ + C₂ + …

Safety Warning — Capacitors

Large electrolytic capacitors in power supplies can retain dangerous voltage (hundreds of volts) for minutes or even hours after power is removed. Always discharge before touching. Connecting a polarized capacitor backward can cause it to rupture or explode violently.

Inductors & Transformers

An inductor is a passive component that stores energy in a magnetic field when current flows through it. Typically a coil of wire, sometimes wound on a ferrite core. The unit of inductance is the Henry (H).

Key Inductor Behavior

  • Opposes sudden changes in current (inductive reactance)
  • When the switch closes: current rises gradually (not instantly)
  • When the switch opens: inductor generates a high-voltage spike to sustain current — called inductive kickback
  • Passes DC, opposes AC (opposite of capacitor behavior)

Inductor Formulas

  • V = L × dI/dt — Voltage across inductor = Inductance × Rate of current change
  • E = ½ × L × I² — Energy stored in magnetic field
  • X_L = 2π × f × L — Inductive reactance (increases with frequency)
  • Series: L_total = L₁ + L₂ + …
  • Parallel: 1/L_total = 1/L₁ + 1/L₂ + …

Transformers

A transformer transfers electrical energy between two circuits using electromagnetic induction. It can step voltage up or down. It works ONLY with AC — alternating current creates the changing magnetic field required.

Parts: Primary coil (input), Secondary coil (output), Magnetic core (iron or ferrite).

Vs / Vp = Ns / Np = Ip / Is

Where V = voltage, N = number of turns, I = current. p = primary, s = secondary.

Types: Step-up (Ns > Np, voltage increases), Step-down (Ns < Np, voltage decreases), Current Transformer (CT — measures high currents safely), Voltage Transformer (VT/PT — measures high voltages safely).

Inductive Kickback Warning

Always place a flyback diode (freewheeling diode) in parallel with inductive loads (relays, motors, solenoids). When power is cut, the collapsing magnetic field produces a voltage spike of hundreds of volts that can destroy transistors and MOSFETs without protection.

Diodes — Introduction

A diode is an active semiconductor device with two terminals — Anode (A) and Cathode (K). Its primary function is to allow current to flow in only one direction (anode → cathode) while blocking reverse flow.

Transistors — Introduction

A transistor is a semiconductor device used to amplify, switch, or control electronic signals. It is the fundamental building block of all modern digital and analog electronics. Invented at Bell Labs in December 1947.

Main Transistor Types

  • BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor): Terminals — Emitter, Base, Collector. Current-controlled device. NPN (most common) or PNP. Used in amplifiers and switching.
  • MOSFET (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor FET): Terminals — Source, Drain, Gate. Voltage-controlled device. Extremely common in digital and power electronics.
  • JFET: Junction FET, used in low-noise input stages.
  • IGBT: Combines MOSFET and BJT features. Used in motor drives and inverters.
  • Darlington Pair: Two BJTs cascaded for very high current gain.
  • Phototransistor: Light-controlled transistor for optical applications.

Must Remember

BJT = current-controlled. MOSFET = voltage-controlled. Both can amplify and switch, but MOSFETs are preferred in digital circuits and power electronics due to high input impedance and fast switching speed.

Integrated Circuits — Introduction

An Integrated Circuit (IC) is a miniaturized electronic circuit fabricated on a single semiconductor substrate (usually silicon). It combines transistors, resistors, capacitors, and diodes into one tiny package, dramatically reducing size, cost, and power consumption.

IC Classification

  • Analog ICs: Signal amplification and processing (op-amps, voltage regulators, comparators).
  • Digital ICs: Logic operations (microprocessors, memory, logic gates).
  • Mixed-Signal ICs: Combine analog and digital (ADCs, DACs, microcontrollers with ADC peripherals).

Common applications: Computers, smartphones, communication systems, audio/video equipment, control and automation systems, medical devices, automotive electronics.

Historical Context

The Intel 4004 (1971) had 2,300 transistors at 10 μm feature size. Modern processors contain over 6 billion transistors at sub-10 nm. Moore's Law predicted transistor count doubles roughly every two years — a trend that held for over 50 years.

Quick Summary — Lecture 1

  • Electronics evolved from vacuum tubes (1906) → transistors (1947) → ICs (1958) → microprocessors (1971).
  • Digital = discrete levels (0 and 1). Analog = continuous range of values.
  • Passive components: Resistors (limit current), Capacitors (store charge), Inductors (store magnetic energy).
  • Active components: Diodes (one-way current), Transistors (amplify/switch), ICs (complex functions on one chip).
  • Schematics show individual components; block diagrams show system functions.
Lecture 2

Characteristics of Electricity: Voltage, Current, Resistance, AC & DC, Conductance, Power

A deep dive into the fundamental electrical quantities that govern every circuit. Understanding these characteristics is the prerequisite for all circuit analysis.

What is Electricity?

Electricity is energy carried by moving electric charges. When there is a difference in electric charge between two points, electrons (tiny negatively charged particles) move through a conductor to equalize it. This movement of charge is what we call electric current.

Why Electricity Matters to Engineers

  • Does useful work: Converts to motion (motors), heat (heaters), and light (LEDs, lamps).
  • Efficient transmission: Can travel long distances through wires with minimal loss at high voltage — one power plant supplies entire cities.
  • Enables communication and technology: Computers, phones, radio, radar, medical instruments all rely on controlled electrical signals.

Historical Engineering Note

The first major U.S. hydroelectric plant was built at Niagara Falls using Westinghouse and Nikola Tesla's AC system. This proved AC could be transmitted efficiently over long distances — launching the Second Industrial Revolution and the age of modern electrical power distribution.

Voltage (Electric Potential)

Voltage (also called electric potential difference, electromotive force, or EMF) is the energy available to move electric charges between two points. Think of it as electrical pressure — the higher the voltage, the stronger the push on electrons.

Voltage Formula

V = W / Q

Where: V = voltage (volts, V) | W = energy/work (joules, J) | Q = electric charge (coulombs, C)

1 Volt = 1 Joule per Coulomb — one volt delivers one joule of energy to every coulomb of charge passing through.

Important Concepts

  • Voltage is always measured between two points — it is a differential quantity, never absolute.
  • We choose a reference point called Ground (0 V) to define all other node voltages.
  • Node voltage: voltage at a point relative to ground (e.g., Va = 10 V, Vb = 5 V).
  • Voltage between two nodes: Vab = Va − Vb = 10 − 5 = 5 V

Electric Field and Voltage

An electric field exists wherever charges create a force on other charges. The field points from positive (high potential) to negative (low potential). Between capacitor plates, the field is uniform. The electric field is the negative gradient of electric potential — stronger fields mean voltage changes more rapidly with distance. Units: V/m (volts per meter) or N/C (newtons per coulomb).

Exam Note

Remember: there is NO absolute voltage. Voltage only has meaning between two points. On exams, "voltage at node A" always means voltage at A relative to a specified reference, usually ground.

Electric Current

Electric current (I) is the flow of electric charge through a conductor. Current is measured in Amperes (A). 1 ampere equals 1 coulomb of charge passing a point per second.

Current Formula

I = Q / t

Where: I = current (amperes, A) | Q = charge (coulombs, C) | t = time (seconds, s)

One coulomb = 6.238792 × 10¹⁸ electrons.

Conventional Current vs Electron Flow

Type Direction Note
Conventional Current (I) + terminal → − terminal (high to low potential) Used in all circuit analysis and schematics
Electron Flow − terminal → + terminal (low to high potential) Physical reality in metal conductors

The conventional direction was defined before electrons were discovered. Engineers kept it for consistency. Always use conventional current direction in circuit analysis.

Useful Analogy

Electrical current behaves like water flow. Voltage is like water pressure — it drives the flow. Current is the flow rate (liters per second). Resistance is like pipe narrowness — it restricts flow. More pressure (voltage) → more flow (current).

Resistance & Conductance

Resistance (R) is a material's opposition to the flow of electric current. All real conductors have some resistance. Unit: Ohm (Ω).

Conductors vs Insulators

  • Conductors (copper, aluminum, gold, silver): One loosely bound valence electron per atom that moves freely — low resistance. Copper is the most widely used conductor in electronics wiring.
  • Insulators (rubber, glass, plastic, air): Tightly bound outer electrons, full valence bands, no free electrons — very high resistance.
  • Semiconductors (silicon, germanium): Resistance between conductors and insulators; can be controlled by doping or electric fields — the basis for diodes and transistors.

Conductance

G = 1 / R

Conductance (G) measures how easily current flows. Unit: Siemens (S) (also called mhos, ℧). High conductance = low resistance = easy current flow.

In parallel circuits, total conductance ADDS: G_total = G₁ + G₂ + G₃…

This makes parallel analysis easier — adding more branches always increases total conductance (lowers total resistance).

AC and DC — Alternating and Direct Current

DC — Direct Current

Current flows in only one direction. Voltage remains constant over time. Sources: batteries, solar cells, rectified power supplies. Uses: mobile phones, laptops, electronic circuits.

AC — Alternating Current

Current periodically reverses direction. Voltage and current follow a sine wave pattern. Generated by power stations. Household electricity (120 V / 60 Hz in North America; 230 V / 50 Hz in Europe).

DC Bias on AC Signals

A DC voltage added to an AC signal causes the entire waveform to shift upward or downward from the 0 V reference — called DC offset or DC bias. Formula:

V(t) = V_DC + V_AC(t)

Positive DC bias: waveform shifts above 0 V axis. Negative DC bias: waveform shifts below 0 V axis. Capacitors are used to remove the DC component and pass only the AC signal (AC coupling).

Sinusoidal Waveform Formulas

  • v(t) = V_peak × sin(ωt + φ) — General sine wave expression
  • ω = 2πf — Angular frequency (radians/second)
  • f = 1/T — Frequency = 1 / Period
  • T = 1/f — Period (time for one complete cycle)
  • V_RMS = V_peak / √2 ≈ 0.707 × V_peak — RMS voltage (effective value for power)
  • V_avg = 0.637 × V_peak — Average value of a half-wave
  • Duty cycle (square wave) = t_on / T × 100%

Must Remember — RMS

RMS (Root Mean Square) is the effective value of AC voltage or current. An AC source of V_RMS = 10 V delivers the same power to a resistor as a 10 V DC source. The household "120 V" is an RMS value — the actual peak is 120 × √2 ≈ 170 V.

Electrical Power & Energy

Power (P) is the rate at which energy is used or transferred. Unit: Watt (W). 1 Watt = 1 Joule per second. Understanding power is essential for component selection, heat management, and energy cost calculations.

Power Formulas

  • P = V × I — Basic power formula
  • P = I² × R — Power from current and resistance (Joule heating)
  • P = V² / R — Power from voltage and resistance
  • W = P × t — Energy = Power × Time (joules)
  • kWh = kW × hours — Electrical energy billing unit
  • Battery life: t = Capacity (Ah) / Current (A)

How Components Interact with Energy

  • Supply: Battery, generator — provides energy to circuit.
  • Store: Capacitor (electric field), Inductor (magnetic field) — temporarily hold energy.
  • Dissipate: Resistor — converts electrical energy to heat (Joule heating).
  • Transform/Transfer: Motor (electrical → mechanical), Transformer (AC to AC at different voltage), Speaker (electrical → sound).

Passive Sign Convention

For a component, if current enters the positive terminal, power is being absorbed (positive P). If current leaves the positive terminal, power is being supplied (negative P). The sum of all power in a closed circuit = 0 (conservation of energy).

RC Circuits — Coupling and Filtering

RC circuits combine resistors and capacitors for filtering, coupling, and timing. A critical concept is capacitive reactance:

X_C = 1 / (2π × f × C)

At low frequency: X_C is large — capacitor blocks the signal. At high frequency: X_C is small — capacitor passes the signal nearly unimpeded.

Typical RC Circuit Roles

  • R1, R2 — Create a DC voltage divider to bias a transistor or amplifier stage.
  • C1 — Coupling capacitor: passes AC signal into the circuit while blocking the DC bias from the source.
  • C2 — Coupling capacitor: passes AC signal to the output while blocking DC from the stage.
  • R3 — Input resistor limiting signal current.
  • R4 — Load resistor at the output.

Engineering Rule: Resistor = controls current. Capacitor = passes AC, blocks DC.

Quick Summary — Lecture 2

  • Voltage (V) = energy per charge. Current (I) = charge per time. Resistance (R) = opposition to current.
  • DC = constant direction and magnitude. AC = periodic reversal, usually sinusoidal.
  • RMS value of AC delivers the same power as the equivalent DC value.
  • Power: P = VI = I²R = V²/R. Energy: W = Pt.
  • Capacitors block DC and pass AC. Capacitive reactance decreases as frequency increases.
  • Conductance G = 1/R. In parallel circuits, total conductance is the sum of individual conductances.
Lecture 3

Laws of Electricity

Master the mathematical laws that govern all circuit behavior — from Ohm's Law through Kirchhoff's Laws. These are the most frequently tested topics in any electronics course.

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law is the most fundamental relationship in circuit analysis. It states that voltage across a circuit element is directly proportional to the current flowing through it, with the constant of proportionality being resistance.

Ohm's Law — Three Forms

  • V = I × R — Find voltage given current and resistance
  • I = V / R — Find current given voltage and resistance
  • R = V / I — Find resistance given voltage and current

Implications of Ohm's Law

  • Applying current through a resistance produces a voltage drop: V = IR.
  • Applying voltage across a resistance produces a current flow: I = V/R.
  • Current always flows from the higher potential (+) terminal to the lower potential (−) terminal — the passive sign convention.
  • The higher-voltage side of a resistor is marked (+), lower side (−). Current enters at (+).

Common Mistake

Ohm's Law applies to linear resistive elements only. It does NOT directly apply to diodes, transistors, or capacitors, whose V-I relationships are nonlinear or frequency-dependent.

Exam Note — Ohm's Law Triangle

Cover the quantity you want to find: Cover V → I × R. Cover I → V / R. Cover R → V / I. This memory trick works every time for basic calculations.

Series Circuit Analysis

In a series circuit, all components are connected end-to-end in a single path. Current has only one path through the circuit.

Series Circuit Rules

  • The same current flows through every component: I_total = I₁ = I₂ = I₃
  • Total resistance is the sum: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + …
  • Voltage drops across each resistor: V = I × R (proportional to resistance)
  • Voltages add up to supply: V_supply = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ (Kirchhoff's Voltage Law)
  • Total power: P_total = V_supply × I = I² × R_total

Voltage Divider Rule

V_x = V_supply × R_x / R_total

The voltage across any resistor in a series circuit is proportional to its resistance relative to total resistance. A larger resistor gets a larger share of the voltage. If R₁ = R₂, each gets exactly half the supply voltage.

Variable output: Replace one resistor with a potentiometer to create an adjustable voltage divider. Replace with a thermistor or LDR for a sensor-based voltage divider.

Exam Note — Voltage Divider

The voltage divider formula given above is for an unloaded divider only. When a load resistance is connected across the output, it forms a parallel combination with the lower resistor, changing the output voltage. This is called loading effect.

Parallel Circuit Analysis

In a parallel circuit, components are connected across the same two nodes — each component has its own separate current path.

Parallel Circuit Rules

  • The same voltage appears across every parallel branch: V_total = V₁ = V₂ = V₃
  • Currents add: I_total = I₁ + I₂ + I₃
  • Total resistance is always LESS than the smallest individual resistor
  • Formulas for total resistance:
    • Two resistors: R_total = (R₁ × R₂) / (R₁ + R₂) (product over sum)
    • General: 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃…
    • Equal resistors: R_total = R / n (n = number of resistors)

Current Divider Rule (Two Parallel Resistors)

I₁ = I_total × R₂ / (R₁ + R₂) I₂ = I_total × R₁ / (R₁ + R₂)

The smaller resistor carries MORE current. The larger resistor carries LESS current. This is the opposite of the voltage divider where a larger resistor gets more voltage.

Worked Example

Two resistors, 20 Ω and 60 Ω, connected in parallel across 30 V supply.

  • I₁ = 30 / 20 = 1.5 A
  • I₂ = 30 / 60 = 0.5 A
  • I_total = 1.5 + 0.5 = 2.0 A (KCL check)
  • R_total = (20 × 60) / (20 + 60) = 1200 / 80 = 15 Ω

Series-Parallel Circuit Analysis

Real circuits combine series and parallel elements. The strategy is to simplify step by step:

  1. Identify purely series groups — add their resistances.
  2. Identify purely parallel groups — apply the parallel formula.
  3. Redraw the simplified circuit and repeat until one total resistance remains.
  4. Find total current using I = V / R_total.
  5. Work backwards through the circuit to find individual currents and voltages.

Engineering Tip

Label nodes with letters (A, B, C…) and redraw simplified circuits at each step. This prevents errors and is expected technique in professional engineering practice.

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

Formulated by Gustav Kirchhoff in 1845. KCL states that the algebraic sum of all currents at any node (junction) in a circuit is zero. Based on conservation of charge — charge cannot be created or destroyed at a node.

KCL — Kirchhoff's Current Law

ΣI_in = ΣI_out

Equivalently: The algebraic sum of all currents at a node = 0. Currents entering the node are positive; currents leaving are negative (or vice versa, as long as consistent).

Example: If I₁ = 5 A, I₂ = 7 A enter a node, and I₄ = 4 A, I₅ = 8 A leave it: I₃ = 5 + 7 − 4 − 8 = 0 A. KCL is satisfied.

Using KCL

  • Identify all nodes in the circuit.
  • Write a KCL equation for each node (except one reference node).
  • Solve the system of equations for unknown currents.
  • Verify: check that the algebraic sum is zero at every node.

Exam Note — KCL

KCL is the tool for parallel circuits and node analysis. For a circuit with N nodes, you need N−1 independent KCL equations. The reference (ground) node is excluded because its equation is redundant.

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

KVL states that the algebraic sum of all voltages around any closed loop in a circuit is zero. Based on conservation of energy — energy provided by sources equals energy consumed by loads.

KVL — Kirchhoff's Voltage Law

ΣV = 0

Sum of all voltage rises = Sum of all voltage drops around any closed loop.

Example: 12 V source in a series circuit with R₁=10 Ω, R₂=20 Ω, R₃=30 Ω. Total R = 60 Ω. I = 12/60 = 0.2 A. V₁ = 2 V, V₂ = 4 V, V₃ = 6 V. Check: 12 − 2 − 4 − 6 = 0. ✓

Applying KVL

  1. Choose a starting point in the loop.
  2. Choose a direction to traverse (clockwise or counterclockwise — be consistent).
  3. Moving through a source from − to +: add the voltage (+V).
  4. Moving through a source from + to −: subtract the voltage (−V).
  5. Moving through a resistor in the direction of current: subtract (−IR) — voltage drop.
  6. Moving through a resistor against the direction of current: add (+IR).
  7. Sum all values and set equal to zero.

Exam Note — KVL

KVL is the tool for series circuits and mesh analysis. For a circuit with M independent meshes, you need M independent KVL equations. Together with KCL, these two laws can solve any linear circuit.

Quick Summary — Lecture 3

  • Ohm's Law: V = IR. The cornerstone of all circuit analysis.
  • Series: Same current everywhere. Resistances add. Voltages divide proportionally.
  • Parallel: Same voltage everywhere. Currents add. Conductances add. Total R is less than smallest branch R.
  • KCL: Sum of currents at any node = 0. Tool for parallel and node analysis.
  • KVL: Sum of voltages in any loop = 0. Tool for series and mesh analysis.
  • Power: P = VI = I²R = V²/R. Always confirm power rating of components before building.

Electric Motors

An electric motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy (rotation) by the interaction of a current-carrying conductor with a magnetic field. The force on a conductor is:

F = B × I × l

F = force (Newtons) | B = magnetic flux density (Tesla) | I = current (Amperes) | l = conductor length (meters). Direction given by Fleming's Left Hand Rule: Index finger = B field direction, Middle finger = current direction, Thumb = force (motion) direction.

DC Motor Key Concepts

  • Back EMF (Eb): When the armature rotates in the magnetic field, it generates an opposing voltage (Lenz's Law). Eb = V − (Ia × Ra). Back EMF is the self-regulating mechanism — as load increases, speed drops, Eb falls, more current flows, more torque is produced to handle the load.
  • Voltage equation: V = Eb + Ia × Ra
  • Torque ∝ Ia × Φ (flux)
  • Speed ∝ (V − Ia × Ra) / Φ

DC Motor Types (by winding configuration)

  • Shunt Motor: Field and armature in parallel. Constant flux → nearly constant speed. Applications: machine tools, pumps, fans. Called a "constant speed" motor.
  • Series Motor: Field and armature in series. Flux ∝ Ia → Torque ∝ Ia². Very high starting torque. Speed drops sharply with load. Applications: electric trains, cranes, hoists. NEVER run a series motor unloaded — it will overspeed and self-destruct.

Motor Safety Warning

DC Series motors must never be started without a load. Without load, flux is minimal and speed becomes dangerously high — the motor can mechanically destroy itself. Always place a flyback diode across motor terminals to suppress inductive kickback when the drive signal is switched off.

Power Supply Systems & Rectification

A power supply converts mains AC (120 V or 230 V, 50/60 Hz) into the regulated DC voltages required by electronic circuits. The linear portion connects directly to the AC line or transformer and uses diodes to rectify AC into DC.

Rectifier Types

  • Half-Wave Rectifier: One diode — passes only positive half-cycles. Output is pulsating DC. Simple but inefficient; large ripple voltage.
  • Full-Wave Rectifier (Center-Tap): Two diodes and a center-tapped transformer. Both half-cycles are used. Requires a center-tapped transformer — not always available.
  • Bridge Rectifier: Four diodes arranged as a bridge. Full-wave rectification without center-tapped transformer. The standard for modern power supplies. Current always flows left-to-right through the load regardless of AC polarity.

After rectification, a large filter capacitor (electrolytic) smooths the pulsating DC into near-constant DC. Then a voltage regulator IC (e.g., LM7805 for 5 V) provides precise regulated output.

Exam Note — Rectifier Rule

In any rectifier circuit: the positive end of the load connects to the cathode of the rectifier. The negative end of the load connects to the anode of the rectifier. This polarity rule holds for all rectifier configurations.

Semiconductors & Diodes

A detailed examination of semiconductor physics and the practical application of diode types in rectifiers, voltage regulators, protection circuits, and signal detection.

Semiconductor Physics

Semiconductors are materials with electrical conductivity between conductors and insulators. The most important semiconductor material is silicon (Si). Silicon has four valence electrons, forming covalent bonds with four neighboring atoms in a crystal lattice.

Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Semiconductors

  • Intrinsic (Pure): Pure silicon. Very few free carriers at room temperature. Conductivity increases with temperature (unlike metals).
  • Extrinsic (Doped): Impurity atoms added to control conductivity.
    • N-type: Doped with pentavalent atoms (phosphorus, arsenic) — adds extra electrons. Free electrons are majority carriers.
    • P-type: Doped with trivalent atoms (boron, aluminum) — creates holes (missing electrons). Holes are majority carriers (behave as positive charges).

The P-N Junction

When P-type and N-type material are joined, free electrons from N-type diffuse across to fill holes in P-type. This creates a depletion region — a zone depleted of free carriers — and a built-in potential barrier (~0.6–0.7 V for silicon).

Biasing the Junction

  • Forward bias: Positive voltage applied to P-side, negative to N-side. Narrows depletion region → current flows freely once forward voltage exceeds barrier (~0.6 V for Si).
  • Reverse bias: Negative voltage to P-side, positive to N-side. Widens depletion region → almost no current flows (only tiny leakage current). If reverse voltage exceeds breakdown voltage, large current flows — useful in Zener diodes, destructive in rectifier diodes.

Must Remember

Silicon diode forward voltage = ~0.6–0.7 V. Germanium = ~0.2–0.3 V. Schottky = ~0.2–0.4 V. LEDs vary by color: Red ~1.8 V, Green ~2.1 V, Blue/White ~3.0–3.5 V. Always check the datasheet.

Diode Applications & Circuits

Half-Wave and Full-Wave Rectification

Diodes are the fundamental AC-to-DC conversion element. The bridge rectifier (4 diodes) is the industry standard — it rectifies both half-cycles without requiring a center-tapped transformer.

Zener Diode Voltage Regulation

A Zener diode operates in reverse breakdown at a precise voltage (Zener voltage, Vz). By connecting a Zener in reverse across a supply rail with a series resistor, the output voltage is clamped to Vz regardless of load variation or supply fluctuation.

R_series = (V_supply − V_Z) / I_Z(min)

Where I_Z(min) is the minimum Zener current for stable regulation (typically 5–10 mA).

LED Current Limiting

LEDs must always have a current limiting resistor in series. The formula is:

R = (V_supply − V_f) / I_f

Where V_f = LED forward voltage, I_f = desired LED current (typically 10–20 mA).

Example: 5 V supply, red LED (V_f = 2 V, I_f = 15 mA): R = (5 − 2) / 0.015 = 200 Ω → Use 220 Ω (next standard value).

Never Connect an LED Without a Series Resistor

Without a current-limiting resistor, an LED draws unlimited current, instantly overheats, and permanently fails. This is the single most common beginner mistake in electronics lab work.

Diode Troubleshooting

  • Test with DMM in diode mode: should read ~0.6–0.7 V forward, OL (open) reverse for silicon diodes.
  • Short circuit in both directions: diode has failed short (often from overcurrent).
  • OL in both directions: diode has failed open (often from overvoltage).
  • LED doesn't light: check polarity (anode = longer leg), check current-limiting resistor value, measure voltage across LED (should be 1.8–3.5 V).

  • Silicon Vf ≈ 0.7 V. Schottky ≈ 0.3 V. LED: 1.8–3.5 V (color dependent).
  • Zener diode = voltage regulator in reverse breakdown.
  • Always use a series resistor with LEDs: R = (Vsupply − Vf) / If.

Amplifier Fundamentals

An amplifier increases the amplitude of an electrical signal. The ratio of output to input is called gain. Small-signal amplifiers operate in the active (linear) region of the transistor's characteristic curves, where small input variations produce proportionally larger output variations.

Gain Definitions

  • A_v = V_out / V_in — Voltage gain (dimensionless ratio)
  • A_i = I_out / I_in — Current gain
  • A_p = P_out / P_in — Power gain
  • A_v(dB) = 20 × log₁₀(V_out / V_in) — Voltage gain in decibels (dB)
  • A_p(dB) = 10 × log₁₀(P_out / P_in) — Power gain in decibels
  • 3 dB point: gain falls to 0.707 × maximum — defines the bandwidth of the amplifier.

DC Biasing — Voltage Divider Bias

The most stable BJT biasing method uses a voltage divider (R1, R2) at the base with an emitter resistor (RE). This provides Q-point stability against transistor parameter variations and temperature changes.

Q-point (Quiescent point): the DC operating point. For linear amplification, the Q-point is set in the middle of the active region so the signal can swing equally in both directions without clipping.

Must Remember — CE Amplifier

Common Emitter is the workhorse amplifier configuration: high voltage gain, high power gain, medium input impedance. The output is 180° out of phase with the input. A bypass capacitor across RE (emitter bypass) restores AC gain while maintaining DC stability.

Amplifier Frequency Response & Bandwidth

Amplifiers do not work equally well at all frequencies. The frequency range over which gain is within 3 dB of its midband value is called the bandwidth.

  • Lower cutoff frequency (f_L): Set by coupling and bypass capacitors (C increases X_C at low frequencies, reducing gain).
  • Upper cutoff frequency (f_H): Set by internal transistor capacitances (Miller effect) and stray capacitances.
  • Bandwidth = f_H − f_L
  • Gain-Bandwidth Product (GBW): For many amplifier types, A_v × BW = constant. Increasing gain reduces bandwidth.
f = 1 / (2π × R × C)

This is the cutoff frequency of an RC network. Below this frequency, gain falls at −20 dB/decade (6 dB/octave).

Capacitor Reactance Revisited

At the RC circuit's cutoff frequency, X_C = R. Below the cutoff, capacitors block signal (high X_C). Above the cutoff, capacitors are nearly a short circuit (low X_C). This determines how coupling and bypass capacitors affect amplifier frequency response.

An Operational Amplifier (Op-Amp) is a high-gain, direct-coupled differential amplifier with very high input impedance and very low output impedance. Quick Summary — Lecture 9

  • Op-amp: differential amplifier. Amplifies (V+) − (V−). Very high gain in open loop.
  • Golden rules (with negative feedback): V+ ≈ V− (virtual short); no input current.
  • Inverting: Av = −Rf/Rin, 180° phase shift.
  • Non-inverting: Av = 1 + Rf/R₁, in phase, very high input impedance.
  • Voltage follower: Av = 1, perfect impedance buffer — connects high-impedance source to low-impedance load.
  • Comparator = op-amp with no feedback. Output is digital (HIGH or LOW), not linear.

The 555 Timer IC

The NE555 is one of the most versatile and widely used ICs ever produced. It operates in three modes:

  • Astable mode: Free-running oscillator — output continuously alternates between HIGH and LOW. Frequency and duty cycle set by R1, R2, and C.
    f = 1.44 / ((R₁ + 2×R₂) × C) Duty Cycle = (R₁ + R₂) / (R₁ + 2×R₂) × 100%
  • Monostable mode: One-shot timer — produces a single pulse of defined duration when triggered.
    t = 1.1 × R × C
  • Bistable mode: SR flip-flop — set and reset pins control stable HIGH or LOW output.

LM7805 Voltage Regulator

The LM7805 is a classic 3-terminal linear voltage regulator providing a stable +5 V output from any input voltage between 7–35 V. It requires input and output decoupling capacitors (0.1 µF ceramic, 0.33 µF and 1 µF minimum per datasheet). Maximum output current: 1 A. Dropout voltage: ~2 V minimum between input and output.

Decoupling Best Practices

Every IC power supply pin needs a decoupling capacitor placed as close as possible to the pin. Recommended: 100 nF ceramic (for high-frequency noise) in parallel with 10 µF electrolytic (for lower-frequency fluctuations). Decoupling prevents voltage spikes and digital noise from corrupting analog signals on the same board.

Practical PCB Engineering

Never share ground return paths between high-current circuits (motors, LEDs, power converters) and sensitive analog circuits (op-amps, ADCs). Use a "star ground" topology or separate analog and digital ground planes connected at a single point to prevent ground loops and noise coupling.

Safety Hub

Electronics safety is non-negotiable. Review these rules before every lab session.

Electrical Safety Fundamentals

  • Current kills, not voltage — but high voltage can drive lethal current through the body. Mains voltage (120/230 V AC) is lethal. Treat all voltages above 50 V AC or 120 V DC as potentially lethal.
  • Always work with one hand when probing live circuits at dangerous voltages — keep the other hand behind your back or in your pocket to prevent current from flowing through your heart.
  • Always discharge large capacitors before working on a circuit that has been powered — they can hold dangerous voltages for minutes or hours after power removal.
  • Use insulated tools when working on live circuits. Never use bare metal tools.
  • Ensure proper earth (ground) connections on all mains-powered equipment.
  • Use a GFI (Ground Fault Interrupter) or RCD (Residual Current Device) outlet in any wet or outdoor environment.

Component-Specific Safety

  • Capacitors: Discharge before handling. Reverse polarity causes rupture or explosion. Never exceed voltage rating.
  • Inductors/Transformers: Inductive kickback can produce hundreds of volts. Always use flyback diodes on inductive loads.
  • LEDs: Always use series resistor. Direct connection to supply destroys the LED instantly.
  • ICs: ESD (Electrostatic Discharge) destroys CMOS and MOSFET gate oxide. Use ESD wrist strap and mat. Store in anti-static bags.
  • Batteries: Short circuit causes extreme heat, fire, and explosion risk (especially lithium). Never short battery terminals. Do not overcharge. Use correct charger.
  • Soldering: Iron reaches 350 °C — causes immediate severe burns. Use a holder. Work in ventilated area — flux fumes are harmful. Wash hands after using lead solder.
  • Motors: Rotating machinery can cause serious injury. Keep clear of moving parts. DC series motor must never run without load.

Troubleshooting Hub

Systematic fault-finding techniques for common circuit problems.

General Troubleshooting Strategy

  1. Observe: Visual inspection first — look for burned components, bad solder joints, wrong component values or orientation, missing connections.
  2. Measure: Check supply voltages at all IC power pins. No supply voltage = nothing works. Simple but often overlooked.
  3. Divide and conquer: Use the block diagram to isolate the faulty stage. Inject a test signal at the input of each stage and measure the output — the stage with wrong output is the problem area.
  4. Replace and test: Substitute suspected bad components one at a time (never multiple simultaneously — you won't know which fixed it).
  5. Verify: After repair, re-test full functionality. Also test edge cases and stress conditions.

Must-Know Items for Every Exam

  • KCL: Sum of currents at any node = 0.
  • KVL: Sum of voltages in any closed loop = 0.

Q1. A resistor of 470 Ω is connected to a 9 V battery. What current flows through it?

Show Answer

Using Ohm's Law: I = V / R = 9 / 470 = 0.0191 A ≈ 19.1 mA

Q2. A 100 Ω resistor has 5 V across it. What power does it dissipate?

Show Answer

P = V² / R = 5² / 100 = 25 / 100 = 0.25 W = 250 mW

Verification: I = V/R = 5/100 = 0.05 A. P = I×V = 0.05 × 5 = 0.25 W ✓

Q3. At a node, currents entering are 4 A and 7 A. One current leaves at 6 A. What is the second leaving current?

Show Answer

By KCL: ΣI_in = ΣI_out → 4 + 7 = 6 + I₂ → I₂ = 11 − 6 = 5 A

Q4. An inverting op-amp has Rin = 2 kΩ and Rf = 20 kΩ. What is the gain? If Vin = 0.5 V, what is Vout?

Show Answer

Av = −Rf / Rin = −20,000 / 2,000 = −10

Vout = Av × Vin = −10 × 0.5 = −5 V (inverted)

Q5. Calculate the series resistor for a blue LED (Vf = 3.2 V, If = 20 mA) powered from 5 V.

Show Answer

R = (Vsupply − Vf) / If = (5 − 3.2) / 0.020 = 1.8 / 0.020 = 90 Ω

Use next standard value: 100 Ω (gives slightly less current — safer for the LED).
